Software Architecture 1

Submitted by Rayhan Muktader on Sat, 07/14/2012 - 16:01

The first step towards building a software with solid architecture is identifying the purpose of that software. Sounds simple enough but being able to discern such things separates you from hoards of bad programmers.

In my teenage years my assumption was that software's purpose is to save people time. It's suppose to do monotonous and uncreative things that are beneath humans. This is a good thesis but it does not encompass the entirety of why we build software. If you look at it from a purist's perspective people who track endangered species or smash atoms aren't concerned with time. So, what is the purpose of building software for them?

My understanding of this matured with experience. Recently, I found that my evolved thesis resonated with what Max Kanat-Alexander wrote in Code Simplicity:The Science of Software Development(O’Reilly), copyright 2012, 978-1-4493-1389-0. I don't dare try to be as eloquent as Kanat-Alexander so I will use some his words to explain my points.

The primary purpose of all software is to help people. You can break this down into a more specific purpose for your individual piece of software. For example, an on-line dictionary exists to help people find meanings for words. An accounting software exists to help people keep track of their finances. A software that autonomously helps the environment exists to help people to help the environment. Software doesn't exist to help computers, it doesn't exist to help buildings or any other "thing".

The primary purpose of software cannot be "make me money". This motivation does not yield optimal result in any creative profession. DeCartes could not have produced something better than "I think therefore I am" if someone paid him a million dollar. He could not have produced it faster either. Here is a link detailing what am talking about. The purpose of software is certainly not to take people's jobs away. New technology shifts jobs and increases net jobs. Otherwise, if we count all the new inventions starting from the invention of the wheel then the net jobs on earth should come out to be zero. If you don't agree with this then you might want to learn about the Luddites and the Luddite fallacy.

Use the purpose of the software to determine the goals of your software. If you are writing a software that allows students to register for courses then your goal is to help students register for courses. You should write down your goal(s) so there is no miscommunication within your team or between your client and you. If your client wants outlandish features then go ahead and humor her as long as those features don't conflict or obscure your goal(s). When you are buried in work prioritize by determining which feature or bug fix will help people the most. If can do this then you have the beginning of a good software that will be appreciated by your client.
